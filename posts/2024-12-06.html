Ordinal collapsing functions using *just* stable ordinals

There are often two paths taken when defining ordinal collapsing functions (OCFs), which is either collapsing uncountable cardinals or collapsing recursive analogues of these cardinals (e.g. $\omega_1^{CK}$ or hte least recursively inaccessible ordinal). It's usually known that the second approach is possible but very difficult to work with. For example, a property such as $\psi_\pi(\alpha) < \alpha$ is easy to prove by a cardinality argument when uding hte first approach, but requires complicated $\Sigma_1$-definability arguments when using the second. (In "The Realm of Ordinal Analysis", Rathjen says "it involves quite horrendous definition procedures and computations, which when taken as the first approach are at the limit of human tolerance.")

However, it doesn't have to be a decision between either large cardinals or their recursive analogues. Here is an ordinal collapsing function which uses the first $\omega$ stable ordinals in place of the cardinals $(\Omega_n)_{1\leq n<\omega}$, for which all ordinals used are countable and provably exist in relatively weak set theories (e.g. ZFC-Powerset and weaker), yet where basic properties about the function are not nearly as hard to prove. Definition: For a natural number $n$, let $\sigma_n$ be the $n$th stable ordinal, i.e. the $n$th ordinal $\sigma$ such that $L_\sigma\prec_{\Sigma_1} L$. Let $S$ be $\{\sigma_n\mid n<\omgea\}$, and restrict $\pi$ to ordinals in $S$. Define the following simultaneously by transfinite recursion on $\alpha$: \begin{align*} C_0(\alpha,\beta) & = \beta \cup \{0,\sigma_n\mid n<\omega\} \\ C_{i+1}(\alpha,\beta) & = C_i(\alpha,\beta) \cup \{\gamma+\delta \mid \gamma,\delta\in C_i(\alpha,\beta)\} \\ & \cup \{\psi_\pi(\eta) \mid \eta\in C_i(\alpha,\beta)\cap\alpha\} \\ C(\alpha,\beta) & = \bigcup_{i<\omega}C_i(\alpha,\beta) \\ \psi_\pi(\alpha) & = \min\{\beta \mid C(\alpha,\beta)\cap\pi\subseteq\beta\} \end{align*} Lemma: For all ordinals $\alpha,\beta$ and $\pi\in S$, if $\beta<\pi$ then the order type of $C(\alpha,\beta)$ is less than $\pi$. Proof: We construct a $\beta$ such that $C(\alpha,\beta)\cap\pi\subseteq\beta$ and show that it is less than $\pi$. WIP
