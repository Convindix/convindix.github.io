A shorter proof of the Paris-Harrington theorem

This is still a work in progress, it may turn out to be incorrect.

$\newcommand{\PA}{\mathrm{PA}}\newcommand{\PH}{\mathrm{PH}}$Andrey Bovykin has a short proof of (a modified version of) the Paris-Harrington theorem in §ion;3 of "Brief introduction to unprovability" (2006, in Logic Colloquium 2006, ed. S. Barry Cooper, H. Geuvers, A. Pillay, J. Väänänen). This proof takes up only one page (page 55 of the above linked PDF), but there is a step at the end which is left as an exercise ("notice that $I\vDash\PA$"). The presentation of this proof (although with a slightly different modification of the P-H principle) given later in §ion;1.2 of his talk handout "A proof of $\mathrm{ZFC}^-$-unprovability of an arithmetical statement, in one step" (August 2016, Rio de Janeiro conference) is one which I prefer, since it keeps the succinctness of the first presentation, while elaborating more on how $I$ satisfies $\PA$. However, using ideas from Silver's work on Erdős cardinals, I think it is possible to get an even shorter proof.

In this post, for a set $S$ of natural numbers and a natural number $k$, although $[S]^k$ is often used to denote the set of subsets of $S$ of cardinality $k$, I will consider the members of $[S]^k$ to be increasing tuples $(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$, where $x_1 < \ldots < x_k$ are from $S$. Exchanging $(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$ with $\{x_1,\ldots,x_k\}$ gives equivalence with the usual convention.

The key difference between this proof and Bovykin's is a slightly different coloring. While Bovykin colors tuples $(a,x_1,\ldots,x_{2e})$ from $[N]^{2e+1}$ with one of only $2$ colors, we will color tuples $(a,x_1,\dots,x_e)$ from $[N]^{e+1}$ with many more than $2$ colors. This way the proof more closely mimics the way indiscernibles are obtained from Erdős cardinals, e.g. three way it is proven in theorem 2.1, ch. 8, §ion;2 of Drake's Set Theory: An Introduction to Large Cardinals (1974). Aside from this, the proof here will also use a different way of looking at the partition calculus. The usual Paris-Harrington principle states that for all $m,e,r$ there is an $N$ such that $N\overset{\ast}{\rightarrow}(m)^e_r$, a condition meaning that a homogeneous subset $H\subseteq[N]$ can be found where $\vert H\vert\geq m$ and $\vert H\vert\geq\min(H)$. Bovykin considers a modified principle, which is the same except for the $\min(H)$ condition, which he replaces with $\vert H\vert > e\cdot 2^{e\cdot\min(H)}+1)$.

The principle I will use, which is here denoted $\PH$, is the same as the previous except the $\min(H)$ condition is replaced with $\vert H\vert > 2^{\min(H)}$ (this condition is subject to change).

We take the following different way of looking at the partition relation: if $N\rightarrow(m)^e_r$, this is the same as saying that for any equivalence relation $\sim$ $[N]$ which partitions $[N]$ into at most $r$ pieces, there is a subset $H\subseteq[N]$ such that $H^e$ lies in one piece of the partition. Given a function $f:[N]^e\to [r]$, a corresponding equivalence relation $\sim$ on $[N]^e$ can be obtained by setting $(x_1,\ldots,x_e)\sim(x_{e+1},\ldots,x_{2e})$ iff $f(x_1,ldots,x_e) = f(x_{e+1},\ldots,x_{2e})$, and the number of equivalence classes of $\sim$ (i.e. the number of pieces of the partition of $[N]$ determined by $\sim$) equals the cardinality of the range of $f$. (I don't count this equivalence as a part of the proof, since as far as I can tell this is a common perspective adopted when talking about partition relations. In fact, Drake implicitly takes this perspective without mention in the middle of theorem 2.1, ch. 8, §ion;2 in his 1974 book.
